  • The float is where the money sleeps Note

    Between "authorized" and "settled" there's a gap — the float. It looks like a delay, but it's really a place where risk, liquidity, and a chunk of the payments business quietly live.

  • SWIFT moves messages, not money Note

    SWIFT is a secure messaging network — it tells banks to move money; it doesn't move it. That single distinction explains a lot about how sanctions and network access become instruments of power.

  • Payments code lives and dies by idempotency 🌱 Seedling

    Networks retry. If "charge $10" can run twice, someone gets charged twice. An idempotency key — a promise that the same request only happens once — is the small idea holding a lot of financial systems together.

  • MDR is three fees wearing one coat 🌱 Seedling

    The Merchant Discount Rate looks like a single percentage, but it's really scheme fees + interchange + acquirer markup stacked together. Whenever someone quotes me "one rate", my first question is which of the three is hiding the markup. The long version is in The Interchange Highway.

    Separate from interchange, paid to the card network itself. Fractions of a cent per transaction — but multiply by every swipe on Earth. Note to self: write up how opaque these are compared to the interchange line.

  • The sub-ledger is the source of truth nobody photographs Stub

    The internal ledger that tracks who owns what, in-flight, before money settles. Boring, invisible, load-bearing. If it's wrong, everything downstream lies. (Cousin of auth vs. capture — both are about money that's promised but not yet moved.)

  • Merchant of Record = who gets the angry email Stub

    Half-formed, but: the MOR is whoever is legally the seller — so they own the tax, the chargebacks, and the compliance headaches. Move the MOR and you move the risk. Want to connect this to MDR, since who's the seller also shapes the fees.

  • Authorization is a promise; capture is the ask 🌱 Seedling

    Authorization earmarks the money ("this card is good for $20"). Capture is the later step that actually requests it — which is why a hotel can hold a deposit for days and never charge it. The gap between the two is a smaller sibling of the float, and it explains a lot of "pending" weirdness. See also How money moves.
