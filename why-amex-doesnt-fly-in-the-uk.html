Why Amex doesn't fly in the UK

🌱 Seedling Created 28 Jun 2026 Last tended 5 Jul 2026

  • Payments
  • Money & Geopolitics

This is the canonical version. Also published on Substack. (placeholder link)

Every traveller has hit it: the little corner shop, or the market stall, or the cab, with the handwritten sign — "No Amex." It's tempting to read this as a status thing, or an anti-American thing. It isn't. It's arithmetic. And the arithmetic is a perfect window into how card economics actually work.

Two shapes of card network

In the interchange essay I described the four-party model: cardholder, merchant, the merchant's bank (acquirer), and your bank (issuer), with a network like Visa in the middle setting the rules. The big fee — interchange — flows from the acquirer to the issuer.

American Express is different. Classically it runs a three-party (closed-loop) model: Amex is the network and your issuer and the merchant's acquirer, all in one company. There's no separate bank to pay interchange to, because Amex is on both ends. Instead of an interchange fee, Amex simply charges the merchant a higher all-in fee — a fatter MDR (Merchant Discount Rate, the total cost of accepting the card).

Why the fatter fee exists

Amex's whole business is built on affluent, high-spending cardholders and generous rewards. Someone has to pay for those rewards, and it's the merchant, through that higher MDR. Amex's pitch to shops is: our cardholders spend more, so paying us a bit more per sale is worth it. For a luxury hotel, that maths often works. For a coffee kiosk on a thin margin, it very often does not.

Enter the regulator

Here's where the UK and EU part matters. Regulators grew uncomfortable with interchange quietly inflating prices for everyone, so they capped it — famously around 0.2% for consumer debit and 0.3% for consumer credit under the EU/UK Interchange Fee Regulation. Overnight, accepting a Visa or Mastercard consumer card got dramatically cheaper for merchants.

But a pure three-party scheme like Amex's has no interchange to cap — the fee is internal. So Amex's merchant costs stayed comparatively high while the four-party networks' costs were forced down. The gap between "cheap to accept" and "expensive to accept" widened, and it widened in exactly the market — price-sensitive UK and European merchants — where every basis point of margin is fought over.

Acceptance isn't a popularity contest. It's a merchant deciding, transaction by transaction, whether the extra spend an Amex customer brings is worth the extra fee Amex charges.

The opinion

My take: "No Amex" is not a snub — it's the four-party model's price advantage made visible. Amex deliberately trades reach for richness: fewer merchants, but higher-value customers and higher margins. In the US, where interchange is largely uncapped, that trade-off is easier to hide because everyone's fees are high. In the UK, regulation squeezed the four-party fees down and left Amex's model exposed — so the trade-off shows up on a laminated sign by the till. If you want to understand a payment system, don't look at the logos on the door. Look at who's allowed to cap which fee.
