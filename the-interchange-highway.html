The Interchange Highway: what really happens in a card transaction

Flagship Created 20 Jun 2026 Last tended 5 Jul 2026

  • Payments
  • Systems & Architecture

This is the canonical version. Also published on Substack. (placeholder link)

You tap a card. A light turns green. It feels like one thing happened. In fact, four or five strangers just passed a message down a chain and, days later, will pass money back up it — and every one of them takes a cut on the way. This essay is the map. Once you have it, most of the payments industry stops being mysterious.

Let me define the players first, because the jargon does real work:

  • Cardholder — you, paying.
  • Merchant — the shop taking the payment.
  • Acquirer (often via a PSP, a payment service provider) — the merchant's bank/processor. It "acquires" the transaction on the merchant's behalf.
  • Card network — Visa, Mastercard, and friends. They own the rails and the rulebook; they don't hold your money.
  • Issuer — the bank that gave you the card and actually holds your funds.

Because there are four distinct roles — cardholder's bank, merchant's bank, and a network in the middle — this is called the four-party model. Hold onto that phrase; it's the hinge of half my other essays.

The two journeys

A card payment is really two trips on the same highway, in opposite directions and on different clocks.

Trip one: authorization (data, seconds)

The tap sends a message — card number, amount, merchant — from the merchant to the acquirer, onto the card network, and to your issuer. The issuer checks funds and fraud signals and answers yes or no. That answer races back. No money has moved yet; the issuer has simply earmarked the amount. This is why a pending charge can vanish: an authorization is a promise, not a transfer.

Trip two: clearing and settlement (money, days)

Later — usually end of day, in batches — the transactions are cleared (everyone agrees who owes whom) and settled (the money actually moves between banks). The merchant gets paid on a delay measured in days, not seconds. And here's the part people miss: the merchant does not receive the full amount.

Where the money leaks: the MDR

The gap between what you paid and what the merchant keeps is the Merchant Discount Rate (MDR) — the all-in cost of accepting the card. It's not one fee; it's three, stacked:

  • Interchange — the biggest slice, paid by the acquirer to your issuer. This is the fee that funds your points and cashback. Crucially, the network sets interchange but doesn't keep it.
  • Scheme fees — the network's own cut for running the rails. Small per transaction; vast at scale.
  • Acquirer markup — what the merchant's processor charges on top, for the service and the risk.

The opinion

Here's my take: interchange is the load-bearing incentive of the entire card system, and most public debate about "swipe fees" gets it backwards. Merchants rail against the networks, but the biggest fee flows to issuing banks — and it exists precisely to bribe those banks into handing out cards and rewards, which is what drives the ubiquity merchants rely on. It's a subsidy loop hiding inside a fee. You can't understand any fight about payments — regulation, rewards, or Europe wanting its own rails — until you see that the interchange highway runs money one way and incentives the other.
