Ten years ago I wrote about BRICS 2.0 — what I got right and what I missed

🌱 Seedling Created 30 Jun 2026 Last tended 5 Jul 2026

  • Money & Geopolitics

A decade ago I wrote a confident young essay arguing that a bloc of large emerging economies — the group usually shorthand-ed as BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa, and the widening cast since) — would build a serious alternative to the dollar-centric payment system within ten years. It's ten years. Grading my own homework in public is exactly the sort of thing this garden is for, so: what did I get right, and where was I wrong?

First, the term I leaned on. De-dollarization is the idea that countries reduce their dependence on the US dollar — for trade invoicing, for reserves, and, the part I care about, for the plumbing: the messaging and settlement rails that actually move money across borders. My younger self treated all three as one trend. That was the first mistake.

What I got right

  • The motivation was real and durable. I argued that the ability to remove a country from the dominant cross-border messaging network would eventually be used as a weapon, and that being on the receiving end would push nations to build alternatives. That instinct held up. Access to the rails became an explicit instrument of statecraft, and the response was exactly the scramble for fallbacks I predicted.
  • Domestic rails would come first. I said the early wins would be national instant-payment systems, not a shiny shared BRICS currency. That aged well — the real successes have been sovereign public rails inside single countries, which then became bargaining chips internationally.

What I missed

  • I confused invoicing with plumbing. Settling more trade in local currencies is not the same as replacing the underlying messaging and clearing infrastructure. You can change the currency on the invoice and still ride the incumbent rails. Real rail independence is far harder and far slower than a headline exchange-rate shift, and I badly underestimated that gap.
  • I underrated network effects and trust. A payment network's value is that everyone is already on it. Bootstrapping a rival means convincing counterparties to hold and settle in something they trust less — and trust, it turns out, is the slowest thing in finance to build and the fastest to lose.
  • I assumed the bloc was more coherent than it is. The members compete as much as they cooperate; "a shared system" glosses over the fact that none of them especially wants to depend on another. A common rail needs a common interest, and I overstated how common theirs was.
The lesson I keep relearning: money moves at the speed of trust and network effects, not at the speed of political will.

The opinion, updated

So here's where I land now, older and slightly humbler: the multipolar payments world is arriving, but through fragmentation, not through a single alternative. Not one BRICS rail to rule them all, but a messier patchwork of national systems, bilateral arrangements, and interoperability bridges — each reducing dependence on the incumbent at the margin without dethroning it. That's less dramatic than my ten-years-ago prediction, and almost certainly more correct. If I write this again in 2036, I suspect the interesting story won't be a new hegemon — it'll be how ordinary the fragmented, interoperable world came to feel.
